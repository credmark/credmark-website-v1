MANAGING RISK AND MAKING MONEY IN UNISWAP

Uniswap is one of the most popular Decentralized Exchanges in DeFi. Its popularity is due to a user‚Äôs ability to earn yield as a Liquidity Provider (LPs). LPs earn transaction fees as other users swap one crypto for another, but like any financial tool using Uniswap comes with a unique set of risks.

Uniswap recently upgraded from v2 to v3 and is encouraging users to migrate liquidity from the older protocol to the newer one. The protocols are different, so naturally their risk profiles are different too. Let‚Äôs examine the risks and rewards of using each version of Uniswap.

RISK IN UNISWAP V2

In Uniswap v2, the biggest risk to a liquidity provider is impermanent loss.

If one of the tokens in a pair‚Ää‚Äî‚Äälet‚Äôs call it Token A‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääperformed well compared to Token B, the liquidity provider would end up with less total value than if they‚Äôd just held the same amount of Token A outside of Uniswap. Lets look at an example between two investors‚Ää‚Äî‚ÄäAlice and Bob.

Let‚Äôs see what happens if the price of ETH doubles.

If ETH doubles, Alice will suffer an opportunity cost called impermanent loss. Impermanent loss happens when the relative price between the two assets changes away from the initial position.

In Uniswap v2, if the price of one asset doubles or halves, impermanent loss goes to 5.72%, as shown in the chart above.

Bob‚Äôs pool drops in value to 707$ + fees, probably mostly generated in ETH, since more ETH had to have been sold than bought in order for the price to drop.

Uniswap v3 has introduced a new concept: concentrated liquidity pools.

A concentrated liquidity pool allows a user to create price ranges they are willing to sell their assets in. The price range is correlation between the two assets. For example, if you set a price range from to 10% to -10%, your pool position would generate profits (from fees) as long as the value difference between the two assets doesn‚Äôt exceed a 10% gain or loss. If you set your price range smaller‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääsay 5% to -5%, you will earn a larger share of the fees within the price range, but the chance of your position going out of this range is a lot higher‚Ää‚Äî‚Ääcausing impermanent loss.

If the prices move beyond the edge of the set price range, the user‚Äôs pool only contains the less valuable token and no longer generates fees. The pool and fees return to normal when prices re-enter the set price range. The narrower the range, the higher the percentage of fees paid to the user.

In short, the tradeoff is with narrower range, the pool is more susceptible to impermanent loss, but generates higher fees. With a wide range, you can avoid impermanent loss, while accepting a lower fee amount.
